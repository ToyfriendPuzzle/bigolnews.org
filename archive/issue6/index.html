Local Faggot Sees God After Being Branded

By: Golden Boy


Deep in Virginia there is magic that dances on your skin and eats into your flesh. It leaves trails on you. There are signs you have been touched by it. 


I trace the marks on my skin that I cannot see but I can feel. They live behind me, so I leave a wake with an impression. 


As awake, so asleep. But when I dream it is of fingers on me. Needles in my flesh. I am tortured by a pain of my own choosing. I have willed torment into creation and I am breathing slowly through the mouth of the beast. He will swallow you whole if you let him. Maybe I’ll let him. 


When I met god, I expected to have a reaction. But no reaction at all is a reaction as well. Stillness in the face of an expanse. Brevity in gravity. Not ease, but not discomfort. Just being is hard when you are used to running. Gasping. Praying on hurt knees on hard floors. There is more to life than existence but in this moment I can do nothing but that. Exist. And I am seen for it. 


Who can see my insides greater than myself? Who will nurse my wounds but me? Do not ask questions you do not want answers to. 


There is magic in deep Virginia, and it is crawling its way towards you on swampy, forested hands. It is asking you the questions you have not asked yourself. 


Maybe if there is a heaven I will ask for your letters on my tombstone and a prayer from your lips on my way out. But maybe god will see what is written on my soul and leave me to this wide, restless maw I have found a home in. Where ‘faggot’ sits just as pretty as the rest of the words I use to keep company. 


Quiet Backroads Always Lead Home

By: Haunted

PC: LogoStudioHanburg



When I was younger, I never understood why someone might need to hitchhike. But now I live in an expensive county where there are no buses, no trains, no taxis, no walking paths, just highway cutting through open land, suburbs split up by fast food chains connected by nothing. 


You can overlook these things when you are used to driving. I spent months going place-to-place in my somewhat new, somewhat shitty car, and returning to my empty home after leaving the crawling expanse of highway. 


But late one night, on my way home, driving through the swampy thick rain that torrents down in this part of the mid-Atlantic, a deer ran in front of my car. I slammed on the brakes, twisting the steering wheel, and hydroplaned off the road. My car came to rest comfortably in the small shoulder, thankfully not spinning out or crashing, and completely missing the steep ditch that was sucking up water maybe 8 feet ahead. Breathing hard, I paused. Maybe I should wait out the rain, because I was less than 20 feet from that deer before I saw it, even with my brights on. My wipers just couldn’t keep up with the downpour. I turned off the car, and suddenly it was darker than anything I was used to. No headlights, no interior lights, just the screen of my phone lighting up my face and not much else as I check the time. A bit after 2AM. I throw on my hazards, so that hopefully anyone else stupid enough to be out in this rain would see me before veering off the road. 


The dark starts to eat at me, as the thrum of the rain fills the air with a drone. The small light of the phone makes me feel vulnerable. Like making a campfire on a deserted mountainside–are you keeping the night out or letting the beasts know that there is warm, comfortable prey nearby? I turn on the interior lights, and set a timer. If it’s not better in 15 minutes, I’ll prepare to leave. 


But I snoozed the alarm the first time it rang, because the dull pounding seemed worse than before, and I could not see anything through the rising mist. And I snoozed it the second time too, thinking that maybe a nap would help me perk up enough for the ride home. I realized I fucked up when I woke up in darkness. I cranked the key in the ignition and just got clicking in return. This isn’t the first time I had drained my battery, so after two more cranks I settled into the knowledge that my car wouldn’t be moving anywhere else tonight. At this time of night, none of my friends would be willing to drive 40 minutes to jump me. I can ask tomorrow, in the light of day, but in the wee hours of the morning I knew I was alone. 


Suddenly, the sweeping silence hit me. I fell asleep to the drone of rain, but now? My windshield was clear, and even though clouds obscured much of the sky, part of the moon still peeked out, illuminating the road around me. I checked my phone, and realized my directions had been on since I parked, and the poor connection has been draining my already shitty battery. 10% charge. I’m a mile from the main road, and 3 miles from home once I’m there. Walkable, maybe, if it didn’t rain again. But what else could I do?


I grabbed my umbrella from under the front seat and stepped into the cool night air. I trudged along the wet shoulder, wishing I had worn rain boots instead of the chunky heels that sunk into the mud with every step. The sucking of mud releasing its grip on me was the only sound around. Everything else was still, as though the rain had washed away the substance that made this a living, breathing place. Bugs and animals washed away, leaving just me and my ever distant car and the many miles of road that led home. I’ve never known quiet that felt so loud. 


As I got closer to the highway, I expected to start hearing the sounds of cars making their way home. But the further I traveled, the less sure I was that I was on the right path. There were no turns, but how could I walk for an hour and not walk a mile? I tried to check my phone again but the screen didn’t wink to life this time. Time doesn’t always pass linearly. Sometimes it loops around in your brain as you experience a moment of infinity. Maybe that is why when I looked back, I couldn’t see the car, or evidence that I had even passed through. And up ahead, there was nothing but more of this overlooked, overgrown road rising up into emptiness. 


When you are the only thing for miles making any noise at all, your ears fixate on it. Too loud, everything in me seemed to whisper. I felt like an animal, caught in the mud and waiting for my ultimate demise. The night stalked me on silent feet as I breathed warm, humid puffs into the increasingly cold night air. The mud soaking my shoes sucked the remaining warmth out of me. So when I was suddenly lit up by headlights behind me, I didn’t hesitate. I turned and tried to flag the car down. 


Mistake 1: Making myself seen. 


The car slowed down until it was inching along. And then it stopped right next to me. It was too dark to make out anything inside, when the car window rolled down. 


Mistake 2: Making myself heard. 


“My car stalled out back there, and I need a ride up to the highway and down south a bit, if you’re headed that way? It’s maybe a 10 minute ride from here.” 


The click of the automatic locks rang out, and the front passenger door was pushed open by an unseen hand inside. I hesitated, but only for a second. And then I got in. 


I couldn’t make the driver out in the dim light. They were quiet, didn’t address me or turn on the radio. But after I buckled, they started moving the car forward, and I watched them shift from first to second fluidly. I stared out the car window, watching the trees slide past slowly and then more quickly as the engine picked up. As it started to rev too high, I heard the sound of the gear shift as the driver put it into third. We were really moving now, faster than I probably would drive on a road still slick with rain and mud, but I was not really in a position to argue. Soon the trees were whipping by and the engine was revving high again. I glanced over, nervous. The driver was still mostly obscured. I could only watch as they shifted into fourth, their gloved hand the only part of them illuminated by the slice of moon shining into the car. We whipped around the curves in the road, and I started to feel motion sickness knotting up my stomach. 


“I almost hit a deer here, earlier, is there any chance we can slow down?” We go careening around another turn and I can almost hear them push harder on the gas. The engine is peaking again, and I watch in horror as they accelerate through another bend and move us into fifth gear. Outside the car is a blur of shadows, as trees whipped past faster than I could comprehend them. Suddenly, I noticed a blur of energy. Alongside the car there seemed to be figures of all sizes, weaving in and out of the trees. At first, they were barely noticeable, but I started to be able to make out racing limbs. Then gnashing teeth. Then wild, blank, roving eyes. Impossibly fast, they seemed to rise up in waves, crashing and crescendoing around the car. I watched in horror as they started to overwhelm the forest around me, a sea of figures pouring out and swallowing the landscape. I could not differentiate one from the other, they were a teeming mass of movement and motion, slinking like an oil slick to cover more of the trees, then the road, then the air just past the window. 


When they started devouring the remaining moonlight, obscuring the last bit of comprehensibility is when I noticed that I’m already audibly muttering, begging, pleading. The words that escape my lips seem to tumble out without any force from me. 


“They move at midnight. They are seeking. They are swifter than light. They do not know fear. They move at midnight. They are seeking. They are swifter than light. They do not know fear. They move at mid–”


It only stopped when the screaming began. I didn’t even realize it was me screaming until later. It was all I could focus on in that moment, the strain and ache of the scream as it echoed through the car. 


“Please, I just want to go home!” I forced those words out as I heard the scream still echoing in my ears. 


And suddenly I was in my car, in my driveway. The porch light was on, illuminating the quiet night. The engine was still bubbling, hot from the drive. I turned off the car and pulled the key from the transmission. I checked my phone. This time it lit up. It was just past 2AM. I got out of the car. I didn’t speak to the shadows as I walked into the house. But I locked the door. 


I don’t drive that road after midnight anymore.
